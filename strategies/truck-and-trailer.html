Truck and Trailer
The truck and trailer strategy is an attacking strategy that involves pre-defining roles at the breakdown in order to speed up the time taken to execute a roll ball. As each roll ball requires the defense to get back 7m, a faster roll ball can leave defenders still running backward, or even left offside, when the next phase begins. Continuously pushing the defence to have to recover quickly over a series of phases can safely win yards, ease the pressure from an aggressive defence and draw defenders into the center.

Here is an example breakdown, where A has been previously tagged. B is the dummy half (going to pick up the ball) and is going to pass to C as first receiver, with D outside them.
At this point, C initiates the strategy by conferring with D. Between them they devise that C will be the “Truck” and D will be the “Trailer”. D moves to stand behind C.

The “Truck” will call for a short ball from the dummy half (B) and will try to time their run so they are running when they receive the ball. Their aim is to make a decent amount of yards and engage the defence, but they are not actively trying to break the line.
The “Trailer” will follow a few yards behind, ready to pick up the ball once the truck has been tagged.

When the truck gets tagged, the key is speed. No over-running thinking you may not have been tagged! The moment you are sure that you’ve been tagged, put the ball between your legs and trust that your trusty trailer is ready and waiting.
Whilst the first “pod” (C and D), are doing the first phase of truck and trailer. The second “pod” (A and B) have had enough time to confer about whether they wish to continue truck and trailering. If they do, B (the previous dummy half) is typically in the best position to take the ball as truck. They should call clearly which side they wish to attack, as they should be able to attack either side. They need to time their run carefully; whilst being fast is good, it’s easy to overrun the dummy half (D) if they’re not quite ready to pass yet.

This strategy can be continued for 3 or 4 phases if desired, with the same 2 pods circulating. I imagine it can make a fairly easy training exercise to just run 4 attacking players up the pitch, going back and forth between the 2 pods until they are used to this enough to commit to it in a game.
Typically this strategy would be more useful when in our own half where we don’t want to do anything particularly risky. Or when it’s wet and we can’t really make very many wide passes. It’s not intended to be used all the time, just a tool to have. As such, it’s imperative to have clear communication so that everyone knows what’s happening. Lots of shouting!
